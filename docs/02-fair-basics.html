Chapter 2 - FAIR basics

FAIR basics

Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  • explain what the FAIR principles are and how they work in practice;
  • distinguish FAIR from openness, documentation, and data management planning;
  • understand what Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable mean conceptually;
  • recognise how FAIR applies to data, code, and other research outputs;
  • understand where FAIR principles are implemented later in the research workflow.

This module builds on the motivation introduced in Chapter 1 – Why Open?. It introduces FAIR as a set of design principles for research outputs, not as a checklist or compliance exercise.

What FAIR means (in practice)

FAIR stands for Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable1.

The FAIR principles describe how research outputs should be structured and described so that they can be:

  • discovered,
  • accessed under clear conditions,
  • correctly interpreted,
  • and reused over time.

FAIR applies across the research lifecycle and to data, code, software, workflows, and documentation, not only datasets2.

FAIR is not: - a requirement to make data open; - a single repository or platform; - a fixed checklist.

Instead, FAIR provides a shared language for designing outputs that remain usable beyond the immediate project.

FAIR is not the same as “open”

FAIR does not mean publicly open.

Research outputs may be: - FAIR and open; - FAIR but access-restricted (e.g. personal or sensitive data); - FAIR with mediated or controlled access.

A common framing is “as open as possible, as closed as necessary”. This distinction is especially important in health, social, and population research and is discussed further in Chapter 8 – Responsible & sustainable research.

FAIR still applies when data cannot be shared

When data cannot be shared openly, FAIR principles still apply to metadata, documentation, and access conditions describing the existence and nature of the output.

Findable: how outputs are discovered

Findable means that research outputs can be discovered by others, including people outside the original project.

In practice, findability is supported by mechanisms such as:

  • persistent identifiers (e.g. DOIs for datasets, ORCID iDs for researchers);
  • descriptive metadata that can be indexed by search systems;
  • stable locations (e.g. repositories rather than personal storage).

Findability is primarily about being locatable, not about being downloadable. An output can be findable even if access is restricted.

Accessible: how access is defined and managed

Accessible means that it is clear how an output can be accessed, under what conditions, and by whom.

Accessibility does not imply openness. Instead, it requires:

  • a clear access route (open, mediated, or restricted);
  • transparent access conditions;
  • persistence of metadata even if the data themselves are withdrawn or embargoed.

From a FAIR perspective, knowing why something cannot be accessed is as important as knowing how it can be accessed.

Interoperable: how outputs can be interpreted correctly

Interoperable means that outputs can be understood and used correctly in different contexts, systems, or tools.

This is supported by:

  • standard or widely used file formats;
  • consistent variable naming and coding;
  • use of shared vocabularies or conventions where appropriate;
  • clear documentation of transformations and assumptions.

Interoperability reduces the risk of misinterpretation when data or code move between people, teams, or software environments.

Reusable: how outputs can be used again

Reusable means that outputs can be used again for future research, whether by others or by your future self.

Reusability depends on:

  • sufficient contextual information to interpret the output;
  • clear licensing and reuse conditions;
  • versioning and provenance information;
  • documentation of limitations, assumptions, and constraints.

Reusability is not about guaranteeing reuse; it is about making reuse possible and responsible.

FAIR applies to more than datasets

FAIR principles apply to:

  • datasets and associated metadata;
  • analysis code and scripts;
  • protocols, analysis plans, and workflows;
  • documentation capturing decisions and provenance.

Applying FAIR principles to code and documentation often delivers the greatest immediate gains in reproducibility and project continuity.

FAIR, data management, and DMPs: how they relate

FAIR principles interact with, but are distinct from, other research practices:

  • FAIR provides design principles for structuring outputs.
  • Data management practice implements those principles through organisation, documentation, and versioning.
  • Data Management Plans (DMPs) describe how those practices will be applied, justified, and resourced.

This course treats FAIR as the conceptual foundation, with implementation addressed in later chapters and supported by institutional infrastructure.

Proportionality and constraints

FAIR is not all-or-nothing.

Projects differ in scale, sensitivity, duration, and resources. Good FAIR practice means making proportionate, defensible choices, documenting constraints, and being explicit about limitations.

This course focuses on FAIR considerations that are realistic for early-career researchers working within ethical, legal, and institutional boundaries.

Where FAIR is implemented later in the course

In this course:

  • Minimum metadata standards are introduced in the Chapter 4 — Data Management Plans (UCL).
  • Planning and justification of FAIR actions are addressed in the Chapter 4 — Data Management Plans (UCL).

The next chapter focuses on project-level organisation and workflows, building directly on these principles.

Exercises (5–10 minutes)

  1. Choose one research output you are working with (data, code, or documentation).
    Which FAIR element (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, or Reusable) do you think is currently weakest — and why?

  2. Identify one constraint (ethical, legal, technical, or practical) that affects how FAIR could be applied to that output.
    Where should that constraint be recorded later in the project?

Key takeaways

  • FAIR describes how research outputs should be structured and described, not whether they are open.
  • Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable each address different aspects of long-term usability.
  • FAIR applies to data, code, documentation, and workflows.
  • FAIR principles inform — but do not replace — data management practice or DMPs.
  • Later chapters translate FAIR principles into concrete project-level actions.
