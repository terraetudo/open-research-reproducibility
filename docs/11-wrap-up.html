Chapter 11 — Wrap-up & next steps

Wrap-up and next steps

Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  • reflect on what proportionate open and reproducible practice looks like;
  • identify which practices matter most in your own context;
  • recognise reproducibility as a form of risk reduction;
  • know where to go next for tools, guidance, and support.

This module consolidates the ideas introduced across Modules 1–10 and points to resources for continued learning and application.

What this course set out to do

This course has focused on practical, proportionate practices that support open and reproducible research in real institutional contexts.

It has aimed to:

  • reduce avoidable errors and inefficiencies;
  • improve clarity, continuity, and defensibility;
  • support collaboration and responsible reuse.

It is not a comprehensive technical manual, nor a checklist to be completed.

Baseline expectations (UCL ECR “minimum viable reproducibility”)

If you do only these things, you will meet a sensible baseline for transparent, defensible research practice (including when data cannot be open):

  1. Project README at the root (purpose, structure, conventions, contacts).
  2. Decisions recorded (decision log or ELN entries for key changes/assumptions).
  3. Raw vs derived separated (never overwrite raw; document transformations).
  4. Re-runnable analysis (scripted steps; basic checks/tests where feasible).
  5. Planned sharing and access (DMP/governance notes + clear access route).
  6. Publish with identifiers and clarity (ORCID, DOIs where appropriate; licence + access conditions; availability statements).

Use Resources and Further Reading for copy-ready templates and UCL routes.

What “good enough” looks like

Reproducibility is incremental.

What is appropriate depends on:

  • project scale and risk;
  • data sensitivity and governance;
  • team size and turnover;
  • available time and resources.

Good practice means making choices explicit and defensible—not maximising openness at any cost.

Practices, not tools

Throughout the course, the emphasis has been on practices rather than specific tools.

Tools will change. Principles such as clarity, documentation, versioning, and transparency endure.

Choosing tools that support these practices in your context is more important than adopting any particular platform.

Reproducibility as risk reduction

Many open research practices function as risk management:

  • documentation reduces reliance on memory;
  • versioning reduces accidental loss;
  • automation reduces silent errors;
  • testing reduces rework;
  • clear publishing reduces misinterpretation.

Seen this way, reproducibility supports both research quality and researcher wellbeing.

Where to go next

Depending on your needs, next steps may include:

  • consulting the Resources and Further Reading chapter;
  • engaging with UCL support services for data, ethics, and open access;
  • attending further training or workshops.

Learning and improvement are ongoing processes.

Applying this in your own work

A practical way forward is to:

  • choose one or two practices to strengthen in your next project;
  • revisit them at key project milestones;
  • build habits gradually rather than all at once.

Small, consistent improvements deliver the greatest benefit.

Exercises (5–10 minutes)

  1. Identify one practice from this course you plan to adopt or improve next.
    What would “good enough” look like in your context?

  2. Identify one risk in a current or past project.
    Which reproducible practice might have reduced it?

Key takeaways

  • Open and reproducible research is incremental and contextual.
  • “Good enough” practice depends on risk, scale, and constraints.
  • Practices matter more than specific tools.
  • Reproducibility supports quality, efficiency, and wellbeing.
  • Resources and communities support continued learning.
