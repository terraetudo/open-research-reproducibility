Chapter 10 — Publishing, PIDs & preparing for reuse

Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • understand publishing as a process of releasing research outputs;
  • explain how persistent identifiers support credit, linkage, and traceability;
  • prepare data, code, and documentation for responsible reuse;
  • distinguish sharing from enabling reuse;
  • recognise how publishing choices affect reproducibility and sustainability.

This chapter builds on Chapter 2 — FAIR basics, Chapter 8 — Responsible research: roles, decisions, and accountability, and Chapter 9 — Automation & simple workflows.

Responsible publishing builds on responsible research

This chapter assumes the principles established in Chapter 8: ethical, legal, and contractual constraints on sharing should be explicit, justified, and documented.

Where outputs cannot be fully open, responsible publishing still requires clear metadata, documented access routes, and appropriate licensing for what can be shared.

Publishing beyond the paper

Research outputs extend beyond journal articles. They may include:

  • datasets and metadata;
  • analysis code and workflows;
  • protocols and documentation;
  • figures, tables, and derived materials;
  • preprints and other early research outputs.

Publishing these outputs supports transparency, enables reuse, and ensures appropriate attribution.

Responsible publishing sits on responsible research practice

This chapter assumes the principles in Chapter 8 (Responsible & sustainable research):
constraints on sharing should be justified, documented, and revisited, and restricted outputs can still be FAIR through metadata, documentation, and clear access conditions.

Publishing as release management

Publishing can be understood as the release of a research object.

A release typically involves:

  • freezing a version;
  • documenting scope, assumptions, and limitations;
  • assigning a persistent identifier;
  • specifying access conditions and licences.

Thinking in terms of releases supports clarity, traceability, and defensibility, particularly where outputs evolve over time.

Persistent identifiers (PIDs)

Persistent identifiers provide stable references to research outputs and people.

Common examples include:

  • DOI (Digital Object Identifier) is a persistent, unique identifier used to reliably identify and locate a research output (e.g. datasets, code releases, preprints, and publications) over time. A DOI looks like this: 10.1371/journal.pcbi.1005510 It acts as a stable reference to an object, even if the web location (URL) changes.
  • ORCID (Open Researcher and Contributor ID) is a persistent digital identifier that uniquely identifies a researcher, independent of name variants, institutional moves, or changes over time. In practice, an ORCID iD is a 16-digit identifier (e.g. 0000-0002-1825-0097) that links a person to their research contributions across systems.
  • ROR ID is a persistent identifier for a research organisation, for example:

https://ror.org/02jx3x895 , (that one identifies University College London).

PIDs allow outputs to be linked across systems and versions, supporting attribution, discovery, and long-term access.

Credit and assessment

PIDs and well-described outputs support credit, attribution, and reuse across systems.
They also reduce friction when reporting outputs for institutional processes and assessment.

Preprints as early dissemination

Preprints are public versions of research outputs shared prior to formal peer review. They support early dissemination, transparency, and feedback, and are now widely used in health and population research.

Preprints should be understood as a stage in the publishing lifecycle, not a replacement for peer-reviewed publication.

Used appropriately, preprints can:

  • make findings visible earlier;
  • establish precedence and credit;
  • invite methodological and substantive feedback.

However, preprints:

  • have not undergone peer review;
  • may change substantially before journal publication;
  • should be interpreted with appropriate caution, particularly for policy- or clinically relevant findings.

Good practice includes clearly labelling outputs as preprints and linking them to subsequent accepted or published versions where available.

Context is part of the output

Reuse depends on context.

Good publishing practice includes documenting:

  • analytical assumptions and decisions;
  • known limitations and sources of uncertainty;
  • constraints on interpretation or reuse;
  • relationships between outputs (data, code, documentation, publications).

Providing context reduces misinterpretation and inappropriate reuse. This includes structured metadata that supports discovery and interpretation, even when access to the underlying data is restricted.

Licences and reuse

A licence states what others may do with an output once they have lawful access. It does not determine who can access an output in the first place.

Publishing for reuse usually involves two decisions:

  1. Access route (open, safeguarded, controlled, mediated).
  2. Reuse licence (what reuse is permitted once access is granted).

Common licence families (what they mean in practice)

Output type Typical licence family What it enables Common pitfalls
Papers, figures, reports, READMEs, training materials Creative Commons (CC) Reuse of text/figures with defined conditions Restrictive CC variants can block legitimate reuse (e.g., translations, teaching adaptations)
Metadata records (dataset descriptions) CC0 (often used) Maximises discoverability and machine reuse Still must not disclose confidential/sensitive information
Code / software Open-source software licences (e.g. MIT/Apache/GPL) Reuse, modification, redistribution of code Licence mismatch across dependencies; unclear copyright ownership in collaborative code
Data Context-dependent (may be CC0/CC BY where lawful) Reuse where sharing is lawful and ethical Governance overrides licensing: you cannot “licence away” confidentiality, consent limits, or contractual restrictions
A proportionate default (often suitable for ECR projects)
  • Text and documentation: prefer CC BY unless you have a clear reason to restrict adaptations.
  • Code: choose an explicit software licence (often MIT or Apache 2.0).
  • Metadata: consider CC0 to maximise interoperability.
  • Sensitive data: publish metadata + access conditions even when data cannot be open (FAIR ≠ open).

Publisher agreements and rights

For journal articles, what you can share (and when) is often governed by the publisher agreement (copyright transfer or an exclusive licence). Record:

  • which version you can share (preprint / accepted manuscript / version of record);
  • where you can deposit it (institutional repository, subject repository);
  • any embargo period;
  • the licence applied to the open version (if applicable).

(See the Resources and Further Reading chapter for links, training and institutional guidance.)

Responsible sharing and responsible reuse

Sharing outputs responsibly involves:

  • respecting ethical, legal, and contractual constraints;
  • choosing appropriate access routes;
  • applying clear licences.

Enabling responsible reuse additionally requires sufficient documentation and context so others understand how, when, and for what purposes reuse is appropriate.

For journal articles and associated outputs, responsible publishing also includes adherence to recognised reporting standards (e.g. STROBE, PRISMA)1, which specify the minimum information needed for interpretation and reuse.
Relevant standards are listed in the Resources and Further Reading.

Many journals now require explicit data and code availability statements. These clarify what is available, under what conditions, and why any restrictions apply.

Preparing outputs for reuse therefore involves a minimum package: clear documentation, structured metadata, an explicit licence, and a statement of access conditions.

Licensing is not the same as access

Licences define how an output may be reused once access is granted.
They do not determine who can access the output in the first place.

For example:

  • data may be openly accessible but licensed for limited reuse;
  • data may be access-controlled but licensed permissively once access is approved;
  • metadata may be openly available even when underlying data are restricted.

Separating decisions about access (open, controlled, mediated) from decisions about licensing supports clearer governance and more appropriate reuse.

Licences should be selected deliberately for each output type (text, data, code), and documented alongside access conditions.

Sustainability and publishing

Well-prepared outputs reduce downstream burden.

Clear documentation, stable releases, and archived outputs:

  • reduce repeated clarification requests;
  • avoid unnecessary recomputation;
  • support long-term reuse without ongoing effort.

Publishing is therefore part of sustainable research practice. Choices about what to publish, how to document it, and where to archive it directly affect reproducibility and long-term sustainability.

Practical guidance for ECR workflows

In practice, preparing outputs often involves:

  • deciding early which outputs will be released and when;
  • using repositories that issue DOIs;
  • linking outputs consistently via ORCID;
  • choosing and recording an appropriate licence for each output;
  • documenting scope, assumptions, and reuse conditions.

These steps support visibility, credit, and robustness.

Exercises (5–10 minutes)

  1. List the outputs from a recent project.
    Which have persistent identifiers, and which do not?

  2. Pick one output.
    What context and licence would someone else need to reuse it responsibly?

Key takeaways

  • Publishing is a process of releasing research outputs, not a single event.
  • Persistent identifiers support credit, linkage, and traceability.
  • Preprints enable early dissemination but require careful interpretation.
  • Licences are essential for enabling lawful reuse.
  • Publishing well supports reproducibility and sustainability.
