Chapter 4 — Data Management Plans

Chapter 4

Data Management Plans as living documents

A Data Management Plan is not just a funder form. It is the written record of what data will be used, how they will be protected, what can be shared, what cannot be shared, and who is responsible.

What this chapter does

This chapter explains how to use a Data Management Plan (DMP) as a practical planning and accountability tool. It focuses on the decisions that need to be made and recorded, rather than repeating the everyday folder and file practices covered in Chapter 3.

The chapter is UCL-aware, but the principles apply more widely. The aim is to help you write or review a DMP that is proportionate, realistic and useful throughout the project lifecycle.

Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • explain the purpose of a DMP beyond compliance;
  • recognise a DMP as a living record of decisions;
  • identify the main areas a DMP should cover;
  • distinguish data description, data organisation and data governance;
  • record roles and responsibilities in a way that supports accountability;
  • explain how DMPs support FAIR, responsible sharing and later reuse;
  • locate UCL and external resources for drafting or reviewing a DMP.

This chapter builds on Chapter 2: FAIR basics and Chapter 3: Project organisation and continuity. Chapter 5 then moves to the internal structure and quality of data files.

What is a DMP?

A Data Management Plan records how research data and related materials will be managed during and after a project. It usually covers what data will be collected or reused, how they will be stored and protected, how they will be documented, what will be shared or archived, what restrictions apply, and who is responsible.

A DMP is not a promise that nothing will change. It is a record of the best current plan, with enough detail to show that data management has been thought through. A strong DMP is updated when the project changes.

A DMP records decisions

A useful DMP should answer:

  • what data will be collected, created or reused;
  • how data will be organised, stored and protected;
  • who is responsible for key decisions and tasks;
  • what will be shared, archived or restricted;
  • why restrictions apply;
  • under what conditions data, metadata, code or documentation can be accessed.

A DMP is the agreement; the project structure is the implementation

Chapter 3 focused on the practical structure of a project: folders, file names, raw-data protection, versioning and README files. A DMP sits one level above this. It explains the decisions and responsibilities behind those practices.

DMP decision Where it appears in practice
Data source and permissions DMP, ethics/governance documents, README
Storage and access controls Approved secure drive, TRE, institutional storage, access log
Raw-data protection Read-only raw-data folder or secure source location
Documentation requirements README, metadata, data dictionary, codebook
Versioning and provenance Changelog, scripted workflows, archived releases
Sharing and reuse constraints Data access statement, repository record, licence, access conditions
Retention and archiving Institutional policy, funder requirements, repository deposit

The DMP does not need to contain every detail of the folder structure. It should state the principles, locations, responsibilities and restrictions clearly enough that the project team can implement them consistently.

Why DMPs matter

DMPs are often introduced as funder or institutional requirements, but their practical value is broader. A good DMP helps a team avoid late-stage uncertainty about storage, access, sharing, retention, responsibilities and costs.

DMPs are useful because they:

  • make data-related decisions explicit;
  • support ethical and legal compliance;
  • reduce ambiguity about access and responsibilities;
  • make FAIR planning more concrete;
  • help identify what needs to be documented or archived;
  • support handover when staff, students or collaborators change;
  • reduce last-minute work at publication or project close;
  • provide evidence that risks and restrictions have been considered.

DMPonline provides a widely used platform for creating, reviewing and sharing DMPs that align with institutional and funder expectations1. The Digital Curation Centre also provides guidance and examples across research contexts2.

A DMP is a living document

A weak DMP is written once and then forgotten. A useful DMP is revisited as the project develops.

The plan may need updating when:

  • a new data source is added;
  • data volume, format or sensitivity changes;
  • access conditions change;
  • new collaborators join;
  • a student or staff member leaves;
  • ethics, governance or contractual approvals change;
  • derived datasets or outputs become important;
  • analysis moves from exploratory to final;
  • the project prepares for publication, archiving or closure.
Updating a DMP is good practice

Changing a DMP is not a failure. It is evidence that the plan is being used as a working document rather than a static compliance exercise.

What a DMP should usually cover

Different templates vary, but most DMPs cover the same broad areas. UCL’s research data management guidance and DMP template provide local support for these issues3–5. External guidance from the UK Data Service, Oxford, ELIXIR and ARDC can also help interpret what good answers look like in practice6–9.

1. Data collection or reuse

Describe what data will be collected, created or reused.

Include:

  • type of data, such as survey data, interviews, sensor data, administrative data, geospatial data, code, images or derived measures;
  • approximate scale, such as number of participants, files, records, variables or expected storage size;
  • whether the data are new, reused, purchased, linked or obtained from a partner;
  • whether the data contain personal, sensitive, confidential or commercially restricted information;
  • what permissions, approvals or agreements are needed.

For secondary data or linked administrative data, the DMP should make clear who owns or controls access to the source data and what restrictions apply.

2. Storage and security

Describe where data will be stored during the project and how access will be controlled.

Include:

  • approved storage locations;
  • backup arrangements;
  • access controls and permissions;
  • encryption or secure transfer where needed;
  • whether data will be held in a trusted research environment or secure setting;
  • who can access identifiable, sensitive or restricted data;
  • what happens when staff or students leave the project.

Avoid vague statements such as “data will be stored securely” unless you specify where, how and by whom.

3. Organisation and documentation

Describe how the data and related materials will be organised and documented.

Include:

  • where project conventions are documented;
  • what README files, data dictionaries, codebooks or metadata will be created;
  • how raw, derived and final data will be distinguished;
  • how versions and changes will be tracked;
  • how software, code or workflow dependencies will be recorded;
  • what documentation is needed for reuse or handover.

The details of folder structure and file naming are covered in Chapter 3. In the DMP, the key point is to record that conventions exist and where they can be found.

4. Ethics, governance and legal constraints

Describe the ethical, legal and contractual conditions that shape data management.

Include:

  • ethics approvals;
  • consent conditions;
  • data-sharing or data-processing agreements;
  • information governance requirements;
  • data protection considerations;
  • intellectual property or commercial restrictions;
  • partner or funder conditions;
  • limits on international transfer or external access.

This is especially important for projects using personal data, NHS data, administrative records, schools data, environmental data linked to individuals, or commercially provided datasets.

5. Sharing, access and archiving

Describe what will happen to data and related outputs at the end of the project.

Include:

  • what data, code, metadata, documentation or materials will be shared;
  • what cannot be shared and why;
  • where outputs will be archived or deposited;
  • whether a repository can issue a persistent identifier;
  • what access conditions will apply;
  • what licence will be used where appropriate;
  • what will be retained internally and for how long.

A good DMP distinguishes between open access, controlled access, restricted access, and metadata-only records. Sensitive or restricted data may still have public metadata, a data access statement, preserved code, synthetic examples or clear access conditions.

6. Roles and responsibilities

Describe who is responsible for key data management tasks and who has authority to make decisions.

This should cover responsibility across the lifecycle: data collection or receipt, storage, cleaning, documentation, analysis, sharing, archiving and project closure.

A DMP should not leave responsibility implicit. If responsibility is unclear, tasks tend to be missed or assumed to belong to someone else.

Roles and accountability

Roles are often where good intentions fail. A project may state that data will be documented, archived or shared, but unless someone is responsible for ensuring this happens, the plan may not be implemented.

A simple responsibility matrix can help.

Task Responsible Accountable Consulted Informed
Data storage and security Data manager / analyst PI or project lead IT / RDM support Project team
README, metadata and codebook Researcher / analyst Project lead Collaborators Project team
Code development and review Analyst / data scientist Project lead Methodologists / collaborators Project team
Ethics and governance compliance Project lead PI / institution Data protection officer / governance team Project team
Data sharing and archiving Data steward / researcher Project lead Library / repository staff Project team

You do not need a formal RACI table for every project. The principle is that responsibilities should be visible and realistic. For small projects, a short section in the DMP may be enough. For larger projects, a matrix can prevent confusion.

Responsibility is not an event

Responsibility is not only ethics approval, governance forms or compliance training. It is also everyday decisions: what can be shared, what cannot be shared, why, under what conditions, and who has authority to decide.

Data description, organisation and governance

A common weakness in DMPs is mixing three different questions.

Description

What data exist? What do they contain? Where did they come from? What do variables, codes and files mean?

Organisation

Where are the files? How are they named? How are raw, derived and output files separated? How are versions tracked?

Governance

Who is allowed to access the data? Under what approvals, restrictions, agreements or legal conditions?

A good DMP connects all three. It does not need to include every technical detail, but it should point to where the details are recorded.

Responsible sharing does not mean sharing everything

Responsible sharing means making clear what can be shared, what cannot be shared, and why. This is especially important for sensitive or restricted data.

A DMP should distinguish between:

  • data that can be made openly available;
  • data that can be shared under controlled access;
  • metadata that can be published even when data cannot;
  • code that can be shared using synthetic or simulated data;
  • documentation that can be shared without disclosing confidential information;
  • outputs that must remain internal because of consent, governance, legal or contractual constraints.
Open does not always mean public

For sensitive or restricted data, good data management may mean discoverable metadata, clear access conditions, preserved documentation, data access statements and secure governance — not public release of the data themselves.

Avoid vague statements

Weak:

Data cannot be shared due to confidentiality.

Stronger:

Individual-level data cannot be made openly available because they contain personal information and are subject to data provider and ethics restrictions. A metadata record, data dictionary, analysis code where possible, and a data access statement will be made available. Requests for access must be directed to [data controller/repository/access committee], subject to approval.

The stronger version is more useful because it explains the restriction and identifies what can be shared.

Sensitive, restricted and high-risk data

Some projects require more detailed planning because the risk is higher. Examples include:

  • personal or identifiable data;
  • linked administrative data;
  • health, education or social care records;
  • qualitative data where participants may be identifiable;
  • geospatial data that could reveal sensitive locations;
  • data subject to commercial licence or data provider agreements;
  • Indigenous, community-controlled or culturally sensitive data;
  • data involving children or vulnerable groups.

For these projects, the DMP should be explicit about:

  • access controls;
  • de-identification or pseudonymisation;
  • linkage and separation of identifiers;
  • secure transfer;
  • approved storage environments;
  • retention and destruction;
  • review of outputs before release;
  • what happens at the end of the project;
  • who has authority to approve sharing.

Planning for derived and intermediate data

DMPs often describe the original data but say less about derived data. This can be a problem because derived files are often the files used in analysis and publication.

Ask:

  • Will the project create cleaned datasets?
  • Will records be linked across sources?
  • Will exposures, indices, scores or classifications be derived?
  • Will geospatial buffers, linkage keys, lookup tables or model outputs be generated?
  • Which derived files need to be preserved?
  • Which derived files can be regenerated from code?
  • Which derived files are sensitive even if they no longer contain obvious identifiers?

For reproducibility, the key issue is whether derived data can be traced back to the source data and code that created them.

What assessors and reviewers look for

When DMPs are reviewed by funders, institutions, supervisors or collaborators, the strongest plans are usually:

  • specific rather than generic;
  • proportionate to project risk;
  • realistic about staffing and resources;
  • explicit about sensitive data and constraints;
  • clear about who is responsible;
  • clear about what will be shared and what will not;
  • connected to FAIR principles;
  • updated when circumstances change.

A DMP does not need to be long to be good. It needs to be clear, truthful and usable.

UCL resources and support

For UCL projects, start with UCL’s research data management guidance and the current UCL DMP template3,4. UCL guidance on managing data across the research lifecycle is also relevant for planning, storage, sharing and retention5. UCL also provides guidance on interdisciplinary data management planning10.

DMPonline can help create plans aligned with institutional and funder requirements1.

Use local support early

If the project involves sensitive data, external data providers, linkage, controlled access, NHS or school data, or long-term archiving, involve research data management, information governance, ethics and library support early. It is much easier to design a workable plan at the start than to repair gaps at publication or close-down.

Activity: review a DMP as a decision record

Individual reflection or small-group discussion

Choose one current or recent project. Imagine you are reviewing the DMP.

Ask:

  • Does it clearly describe the data sources?
  • Does it say where data are stored and who can access them?
  • Does it explain what documentation will be created?
  • Does it state what can be shared, what cannot be shared, and why?
  • Does it identify who is responsible for storage, documentation, analysis, sharing and archiving?
  • Does it explain when the DMP will be reviewed or updated?

Identify one section that is too vague and rewrite it as a specific decision.

Activity: constraints and access

Make restrictions visible

Identify one constraint that limits what can be shared openly. This might be ethical, legal, contractual, governance-related or practical.

Then write a short access statement using this structure:

  1. What cannot be shared?
  2. Why not?
  3. What can be shared instead?
  4. Who can be contacted, or what process applies, if access is possible?
  5. Where is this information recorded?

Key takeaways

  • A DMP is a living record of data-management decisions, not just a compliance form.
  • The DMP records the agreement; the project structure implements it.
  • Good DMPs are specific, proportionate and realistic.
  • Roles and responsibilities should be visible, not assumed.
  • Responsible sharing means explaining what can be shared, what cannot, why, and under what conditions.
  • Sensitive or restricted data can still be managed in FAIR, transparent and reproducible ways.
