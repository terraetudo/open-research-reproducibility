Chapter 8 — Responsible research: roles, decisions, and accountability

Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  • explain responsibility as an ongoing research practice;
  • recognise ethical, legal, and governance considerations in everyday workflows;
  • identify and articulate roles and responsibilities within a research project;
  • document constraints, decisions, and trade-offs transparently;
  • link responsibility to decisions about sharing, restriction, and reuse.

This module builds on Chapter 7 — Code quality & testing and informs later decisions about Chapter 10 — Publishing, PIDs & preparing for reuse.

What responsibility means in practice

Responsible research is not limited to formal ethics approval or compliance checkpoints.

It involves anticipating consequences, making proportionate choices, and recording decisions throughout the project lifecycle.

Most responsibility challenges arise not from exceptional cases, but from routine decisions that are undocumented, implicit, or assumed to be someone else’s responsibility.

Responsibility as anticipation and decision capture

The Turing Way frames responsibility as anticipatory.

Key questions include:

  • Who might be affected by this research?
  • What risks, burdens, or exclusions could arise?
  • What constraints limit what can be done, shared, or reused?

Responsible practice requires that these considerations are made explicit and recorded, alongside the rationale for decisions taken.

Documenting constraints and trade-offs allows decisions to be understood, revisited, and defended as contexts change.

Roles, responsibilities, and accountability

Responsibility in research is distributed.

Projects involve multiple roles, which may overlap and change over time. Examples include:

  • principal investigator or project lead;
  • supervisors and advisory roles;
  • analysts and data stewards;
  • collaborators and external partners;
  • institutional approvers and governance bodies.

Unclear or assumed responsibilities are a common source of risk. When roles are not explicit, decisions may be delayed, duplicated, or left undocumented.

Responsible research therefore requires:

  • identifying who is responsible for key decisions;
  • recognising where responsibilities overlap;
  • recording how decisions are made and by whom.

This is particularly important for:

  • data access and governance;
  • ethics and regulatory approvals;
  • analytical choices and exclusions;
  • decisions about sharing, restriction, and reuse.

Responsibility is not about assigning blame. It is about ensuring that accountability is visible, supported, and reviewable.

Equity, inclusion, and accessibility as responsibility

Equity and accessibility are not optional add-ons. They are operational aspects of responsible practice.

Examples include:

  • tools or workflows that require specialist infrastructure;
  • documentation that assumes prior technical or institutional knowledge;
  • processes that place hidden burdens on junior staff or collaborators.

Making expectations explicit, documenting decisions, and choosing proportionate approaches improves inclusion and collaboration.

Responsibility in sharing, restriction, and reuse

Responsible research extends to how outputs are shared and reused.

This includes:

  • setting clear access conditions and licences;
  • avoiding over-claiming reuse where constraints apply;
  • providing sufficient context to prevent misinterpretation.

These considerations link directly to Chapter 10 — Publishing, PIDs & preparing for reuse.

Responsible sharing does not mean sharing everything. It means being explicit about what can be shared, what cannot, and why.

Institutional support and escalation routes

Responsibility is supported by institutional infrastructure.

At UCL, guidance and support are available for:

  • research ethics and governance;
  • data protection and information security;
  • research integrity and responsible conduct;
  • data management, sharing, and preservation.

Knowing when and where to seek advice is itself a responsible practice.

Relevant training, policies, and support routes are collated in the Resources and Further Reading.

Practical guidance for ECR workflows

For early-career researchers, proportionate responsible practice often involves:

  • clarifying roles and expectations early in a project;
  • documenting constraints and trade-offs rather than hiding them;
  • recording decisions as they are made, not retrospectively;
  • seeking advice when boundaries or responsibilities are unclear.

Responsibility is a shared, supported practice — not an individual burden.

Exercises (5–10 minutes)

  1. Identify one decision in a current project.
    Who was responsible, and is the rationale documented?

  2. Identify one constraint (ethical, legal, practical).
    Is it recorded clearly enough for someone new to the project to understand?

Key takeaways

  • Responsibility is an ongoing, documented practice.
  • Clear roles and accountability reduce risk and confusion.
  • Constraints and trade-offs should be recorded, not hidden.
  • Responsible sharing requires transparency about limits.
  • Institutional support is part of responsible research.
