Chapter 9 — Wrap-up and next steps

Chapter 9

Wrap-up and next steps

Open and reproducible research is not a single tool, a single repository, or a final task before submission. It is a set of practical habits that make research easier to understand, check, maintain and reuse.

What this chapter does

This final chapter brings the workshop together. It summarises the core message of the course, revisits the main practices covered across the chapters, and helps you identify a realistic next step for your own work.

The aim is not to leave with a perfect workflow. The aim is to leave with a clearer sense of what matters most, where your current risks are, and which small changes would make your research more transparent, defensible and sustainable.

Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • reflect on what proportionate open and reproducible practice looks like;
  • identify which practices matter most in your own context;
  • recognise reproducibility as a form of risk reduction;
  • distinguish between practices that are essential now and practices that can develop over time;
  • know where to go next for tools, guidance and support.

This chapter consolidates the ideas introduced across the workshop and points to the Resources and further reading chapter for continued learning.

What this workshop set out to do

This workshop has focused on practical, proportionate practices that support open and reproducible research in real institutional contexts.

It aimed to help you:

  • reduce avoidable errors and inefficiencies;
  • improve clarity, continuity and defensibility;
  • support collaboration and handover;
  • make responsible decisions about sharing and restriction;
  • prepare outputs so that others can find, cite, understand and reuse them where appropriate.

It is not a comprehensive technical manual. It is also not a compliance checklist to be completed once and forgotten. Instead, it is a framework for improving everyday research practice.

The central message

Reproducibility is not perfection. It is making the research process visible enough that someone else — including your future self — can understand what was done, why it was done, and how the results were produced.

The story of the workshop

The workshop followed a workflow logic.

1. Why it matters

We started with open research and reproducibility: why they matter, what they mean, and why they are not the same as making everything public.

2. Good structure

We used FAIR principles and data integrity concepts to think about how outputs can be findable, accessible, interoperable and reusable.

3. Project organisation

We considered how folder structures, file naming, project-level READMEs and lightweight versioning make research easier to navigate.

4. Data management planning

We treated the DMP as a living record of decisions about data, roles, storage, protection, sharing and restrictions.

5. Tidy and analysable data

We looked at the structure of the data file itself: tidy data, spreadsheet risks, missingness, exclusions and data dictionaries.

6. Documentation and protocols

We focused on keeping decisions visible through READMEs, metadata, decision logs, changelogs, SOPs, protocols and reporting guidance.

7. Coding for reproducibility

We brought together readable code, checks, testing, versioning, light automation and sustainable computing.

8. Publishing and reuse

We considered repositories, persistent identifiers, preprints, licences, access statements and preparing outputs for responsible reuse.

The common thread is simple: make the route visible.

Minimum viable reproducibility

Not every project needs the same level of infrastructure. A small student project, a linked administrative data study, a multi-site collaboration and a policy-facing evaluation will have different levels of risk, sensitivity and complexity.

However, most projects benefit from a small set of baseline practices.

Minimum viable reproducibility

If you do only a few things, make them these:

  1. Project README at the root
    Explain the project purpose, structure, conventions, contacts and key restrictions.

  2. Raw and derived data separated
    Never overwrite raw data. Document how derived datasets were created.

  3. Decisions recorded
    Keep a decision log, ELN entry or equivalent record of key assumptions, exclusions, changes and deviations.

  4. Re-runnable analysis
    Use scripts or Quarto/R Markdown files rather than manual steps where possible.

  5. Basic checks included
    Check files, variables, missingness, IDs and implausible values before analysis.

  6. Planned sharing and access
    Record what can be shared, what cannot, why, and under what conditions.

  7. Outputs prepared for citation and reuse
    Use ORCID, DOIs where appropriate, licences, access statements and clear metadata.

These practices are deliberately modest. They are not the end point, but they are a strong baseline for transparent and defensible research.

What “good enough” looks like

Reproducibility is incremental.

What is appropriate depends on:

  • project scale and complexity;
  • data sensitivity and governance;
  • team size and turnover;
  • expected reuse;
  • available time and resources;
  • funder, journal and institutional requirements.

Good practice means making choices explicit and defensible, not maximising openness at any cost.

For sensitive or restricted data, “good enough” may mean:

  • clear metadata rather than open data;
  • documented access conditions rather than public download;
  • synthetic examples rather than confidential records;
  • preserved code that can run in a secure environment;
  • transparent explanation of what cannot be shared and why.

For low-risk open data projects, “good enough” may mean:

  • data in a trusted repository;
  • a DOI;
  • a README;
  • a data dictionary;
  • an explicit licence;
  • analysis code linked to the publication.
Proportionate practice

Ask: What would someone need to understand, check or reuse this work appropriately?
Then ask: What is realistic and responsible in this context?

Practices, not tools

Throughout the workshop, the emphasis has been on practices rather than specific tools.

Tools change. Research contexts differ. What matters is whether the tool supports a clear practice.

Practice Possible tools
Project organisation RStudio projects, folder templates, shared drives, GitHub repositories
Documentation README files, Markdown, Word documents, ELNs, SOPs
Decision capture Decision logs, lab notebooks, issue trackers, changelogs
Coding and checking R, Python, Quarto, R Markdown, Jupyter, validation scripts
Versioning Git, GitHub, changelogs, dated releases
Publishing and citation Institutional repositories, Zenodo, OSF, DataCite DOIs, ORCID

The tool is useful only if it makes the practice easier to sustain.

Reproducibility as risk reduction

Many open research practices function as risk management.

Risk Reproducible practice that reduces it
No one knows which file is current Folder structure, file naming, versioning
A key team member leaves README, onboarding notes, decision logs
Analysis cannot be rerun Scripted workflows, project-relative paths, environment notes
Silent data errors affect results Validation checks, missingness summaries, tests
Data cannot be shared openly Metadata, access statements, controlled-access routes
Others misuse or misinterpret outputs Documentation, licences, context and limitations
Code is abandoned or rewritten Readable code, functions, Git, clear dependencies
Outputs cannot be credited DOIs, ORCID, citation guidance

Seen this way, reproducibility supports research quality, efficiency and researcher wellbeing. It reduces the mental load of remembering everything and reduces the risk of having to reconstruct decisions under pressure.

What to do first

The best next step is usually small and specific.

Avoid trying to fix the whole workflow at once. Pick one current project and choose one improvement that reduces a real risk.

Activity: choose one next action

Think about a current or recent project.

Choose one action you could take in the next one or two weeks.

Examples:

  • create or update the project README;
  • write down the folder structure and naming convention;
  • separate raw and derived data;
  • add a short decision log;
  • add basic checks to a script;
  • record package versions;
  • create a data dictionary;
  • draft a data/code availability statement;
  • identify a suitable repository;
  • add a licence to training materials or code.

A good next action is:

  • specific;
  • realistic;
  • visible to the project team;
  • linked to a real source of risk;
  • small enough to complete.

A personal reproducibility plan

Use the prompts below to create a short plan.

Personal action plan

1. One thing I will keep doing

What already works well in your current workflow?

2. One thing I will stop doing

What creates confusion, duplication or avoidable risk?

3. One thing I will start doing

What small habit would make the biggest difference?

4. One person I need to involve

Who needs to agree, know about, or support this change?

5. One place I will record it

Where will this decision or action be documented?

This can be used individually, in pairs, or as a final workshop discussion.

For teams: make it social

Reproducibility is easier when it is a shared expectation rather than an individual burden.

For teams, useful next steps include:

  • agreeing a default folder structure;
  • agreeing file naming conventions;
  • creating a project README template;
  • deciding where decision logs live;
  • adding documentation checks to onboarding and offboarding;
  • reviewing data management plans at project milestones;
  • making code review or script walkthroughs normal;
  • agreeing where outputs will be archived or published.

Small team agreements reduce repeated negotiation and make expectations clearer for students, new staff and collaborators.

Reproducibility is a team culture

If only one person knows how the project works, the project is fragile. Shared conventions and visible decisions make the work more resilient.

What support is available?

You do not need to solve everything alone.

Depending on the issue, useful support may include:

  • research data management teams;
  • library open research and open access teams;
  • ethics and governance support;
  • data protection and information governance teams;
  • research software engineering or advanced research computing support;
  • departmental open science champions;
  • institutional repository support;
  • journal and funder guidance;
  • peer communities such as The Turing Way, The Carpentries and UKRN.

Use the Resources and further reading chapter as a starting point. It gathers practical guidance, templates and training routes.

What this workshop does not solve

It is important to be honest about the limits of individual practice.

Reproducibility problems are also shaped by:

  • incentives and assessment systems;
  • workload and time pressure;
  • publication bias;
  • unequal access to support and infrastructure;
  • data governance complexity;
  • short-term contracts and staff turnover;
  • underinvestment in research software, documentation and stewardship.

The practices covered here do not fix all of these structural problems. They do, however, help you reduce avoidable risk in your own projects and provide a basis for advocating for better systems, support and recognition.

Final reflection

Final reflection

  • The most useful thing I will take from this workshop is…
  • One risk I can now see more clearly is…

  • One thing I need from my team or institution is…

Key takeaways

  • Open and reproducible research is incremental and contextual.
  • “Good enough” practice depends on risk, scale and constraints.
  • Practices matter more than specific tools.
  • Reproducibility reduces risk, improves continuity and supports responsible reuse.
  • Small, consistent improvements often deliver the greatest benefit.
  • Resources, teams and communities can support continued learning.

Thank you

Thank you for taking part in the Open Research and Reproducibility workshop.

The next step is not to make every project perfect. The next step is to make one part of your workflow clearer, safer and easier to understand.

Start small. Make it visible. Build from there.
