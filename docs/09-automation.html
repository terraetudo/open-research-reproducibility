Chapter 9 — Automation & simple workflows

Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  • explain why automation supports reproducibility and efficiency;
  • recognise which steps benefit most from automation;
  • understand automation as dependency management;
  • link automation to reduced compute waste and sustainability;
  • apply proportionate automation in research workflows.

This module builds on Chapter 7 — Code quality & testing and Chapter 8 — Responsible research: roles, decisions, and accountability. It prepares you for Chapter 10 — Publishing, PIDs & preparing for reuse.

What automation means in research

In research, automation does not mean complex pipelines or advanced tooling by default.

Automation means making the steps that turn inputs (data, parameters, code) into outputs (tables, figures, models, results) explicit, repeatable, and runnable without manual intervention.At its core, automation is a way of managing dependencies between inputs, transformations, and outputs.

A workflow can be automated even if it consists of only a small number of scripts, as long as:

  • the execution order is clear;
  • inputs and outputs are explicit;
  • results can be regenerated reliably.

Automation sits above individual scripts.
Code quality focuses on how code is written (Chapter 7); automation focuses on how pieces of work fit together.

Why automate research workflows?

Manual workflows are fragile.

They rely on undocumented assumptions, memory, and manual steps that are difficult to reconstruct later — especially by collaborators, reviewers, or your future self.

Automation helps ensure that:

  • steps are run in the correct order;
  • inputs and outputs are clearly linked;
  • analyses can be re-run consistently.

This reduces error, supports review, and increases confidence that results are defensible and reproducible.By avoiding unnecessary full reruns, automation reduces compute waste and supports environmentally sustainable research practice.

For open research, automation is often the difference between sharing code and enabling reuse.

Automation as dependency management

At its core, automation is about dependencies:

  • which files or datasets are inputs;
  • which scripts transform them;
  • which outputs depend on which steps.

When dependencies are explicit, workflows can:

  • rerun only what has changed;
  • avoid accidental full reprocessing;
  • make provenance easier to inspect and explain.

Simple automation relies on good foundations: clear folder structure, stable file naming, versioning, and separation of raw and derived data, as introduced in Chapter 5 — Organising and tidying data.

What to automate (and what not to)

Automation is most valuable when steps are routine, repeatable, and diagnostic.

In health and population research, many activities labelled as “exploratory” are in fact systematic checks that recur across datasets and projects. These are prime candidates for automation.

Strong candidates for automation

Automate steps that are:

  • Repeated across projects or data extracts, such as:
    • missingness summaries and patterns;
    • variable ranges and implausible values;
    • consistency checks (e.g. dates, identifiers, categories);
    • duplicate detection and basic record counts.
  • Required every time data are refreshed or updated, including:
    • re-running descriptive summaries;
    • regenerating standard tables or plots;
    • validating joins or linkage outputs.
  • Costly to rerun manually if missed, where late discovery leads to downstream rework.

Automating these steps improves comparability, creates an audit trail, and reduces the risk that important checks are skipped.

What should remain flexible

Not all exploratory work should be fully automated.

Activities that often benefit from flexibility include:

  • hypothesis generation and ad-hoc visualisation;
  • one-off transformations explored to understand unusual patterns;
  • conceptual restructuring of variables or outcomes.

However, even here, partial automation is often beneficial: saving scripts, parameterising functions, or re-running the same exploratory logic on updated data.

The goal is not to constrain thinking, but to avoid repeating the same diagnostic work from scratch.

Automation and sustainability

Automation supports sustainability by reducing waste.

Well-designed workflows:

  • avoid repeated full pipeline runs;
  • reuse unchanged intermediate results;
  • prevent accidental recomputation.

From a sustainability perspective, automation operationalises the green coding principles introduced in Chapter 7 — Code quality & testing.

Reducing unnecessary computation saves time, energy, and storage — and improves reproducibility at the same time.

Levels of automation (proportionate choices)

Automation can be lightweight.

Common forms include:

  • scripts that run analyses end-to-end;
  • simple rule-based pipelines (e.g. make-style logic);
  • scheduled or triggered runs for key steps.

The appropriate level depends on project scale, team size, and computational cost. More complex tooling is optional, not mandatory.

Starting small and scaling only when needed is usually the most sustainable choice.

Transparency and automation

Automated workflows support transparency by:

  • documenting execution order implicitly;
  • making assumptions and dependencies explicit;
  • producing consistent, traceable outputs.

Automation complements documentation — it does not replace it. Clear READMEs, comments, and decision logs remain essential (see Chapter 6).

Practical guidance for ECR workflows

For most research projects, proportionate automation involves:

  • writing scripts that can be run from top to bottom;
  • avoiding manual edits to generated files;
  • recording when and why full reruns are required;
  • adding automation incrementally as projects mature.

You do not need complex tools to gain most of the benefits. Consistency and clarity matter more than sophistication.

Examples, tools, and further guidance are collated in the Resources and Further Reading chapter.

Exercises (5–15 minutes)

  1. Identify one repeated manual step in a current project.
    Could it be automated with a single script?

  2. Pick one analysis output.
    Can you trace which inputs and scripts produced it?

Key takeaways

  • Automation supports reproducibility, transparency, and efficiency.
  • Managing dependencies is the core benefit of automation.
  • Not all steps need to be automated.
  • Automation reduces compute waste and supports sustainability.
  • Start simple and scale only when needed.
