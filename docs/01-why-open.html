Chapter 1 - Why Open?

Why Open?

Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you should be able to:

  • explain why openness and reproducibility are now core expectations in research;
  • distinguish between openness, transparency, and reproducibility in practical terms;
  • recognise common sources of irreproducibility in everyday research workflows;
  • understand how open practices reduce research risk, inefficiency, and avoidable error;
  • see how the principles introduced here translate into concrete practices covered in later modules.

This module sets the conceptual foundation for the rest of the course. The next module moves from why openness matters to how to describe and structure outputs so they remain usable over time (Chapter 2: FAIR basics).

Why openness now?

Open research is no longer a niche movement or an optional add-on. Across scientific research, concerns about irreproducible findings, undocumented analytical decisions, and inaccessible research outputs have led funders, journals, and institutions to raise expectations around transparency and robustness1–3

In practice, this shift is less about ideology and more about reducing avoidable risk: rework, errors, lost context, and fragile results that cannot be checked, reused, or defended. This course is designed to support proportionate improvements that fit real research constraints.

Open and reproducible research supports meaningful involvement of patients, participants, and communities by making assumptions, decisions, and limitations visible and contestable.

Key terms (used consistently in this course)

These terms overlap, but they are not identical:

  • Open research: making research outputs, methods, and decisions accessible to others, where lawful, ethical, and appropriate.
  • Transparency: clearly documenting what was done, why it was done, and how decisions were made (including decisions not visible in the final paper).
  • Reproducibility: enabling someone else (or you in six months) to understand and, where possible, recreate the analytical steps and results using the same data and methods.

A project can be reproducible without being fully open (e.g. sensitive data). Conversely, sharing outputs is not sufficient for reproducibility if key steps or provenance are missing.

Reproducible is not the only “-ible”

In The Turing Way terminology, results may be described as:

  • reproducible (same data, same analysis),
  • replicable (different data, same analysis),
  • robust (same data, different analysis),
  • generalisable (different data, different analysis).

These distinctions are useful when deciding what level of checking or reuse is realistic for a given project. For a short explainer and visual summary, see The Turing Way Definitions page4.

How to read this course

This course separates principles, practices, and tools:

  • Principles are the underlying aims (e.g. transparency, accountability, reuse).
  • Practices are the behaviours that implement those aims (e.g. versioning, documentation, sharing decisions).
  • Tools are the platforms that support practices (e.g. repositories, version control systems, workflow tools).

Tools change quickly; principles and practices travel well across projects and roles. This is why the course emphasises practices, while the Resources and Further Reading chapter centralises tool- and UCL-specific signposting.

Minimum expectations in practice (UCL and UK funders)

Open research is treated as a default position, applied across the research lifecycle and implemented proportionately within ethical, legal, and contractual constraints.

In most UCL research contexts, you should assume the following baseline expectations apply somewhere in your project:

  • Plan data management proportionately, and document decisions (a Data Management Plan may be required by funders and is generally expected for funded projects and projects handling research data).
  • Make outputs findable and preservable: ensure data, code, protocols, and other outputs (or at least metadata describing them) can be located, interpreted, and retained appropriately.
  • Enable open access to publications through the institutional route, in line with funder and assessment requirements.
  • Record constraints explicitly (e.g. sensitive data, governance restrictions, IP, contractual limits) and document how they affect sharing, access, and retention.

This course shows how to meet these expectations through robust everyday practice. UCL-facing routes, contacts, and templates are maintained in Resources and Further Reading.

Where irreproducibility comes from (in normal workflows)

Irreproducibility is usually not caused by misconduct. More commonly it arises from routine workflow issues, including:

  • manual data cleaning or analysis steps that are not recorded;
  • unclear file organisation, naming, or versioning;
  • results generated interactively without a saved script or pipeline;
  • missing information about software environments or dependencies;
  • loss of project memory when staff or students move on.

These problems tend to surface late (submission, peer review, audit, or handover), when they are most expensive to fix.

Proportionality and constraints

Open and reproducible practice is not a one-size-fits-all checklist. Constraints may include personal or sensitive data, contractual obligations, intellectual property considerations, and limited time or resource.

Good practice means making constraints explicit, documenting decisions, and using appropriate infrastructure. Proportionality applies to how you implement open practices, not to whether you document and justify decisions.

Open research in practice: what this implies for day-to-day work

Open research is not only about making outputs publicly available at the end of a project. Across the research lifecycle, work should be organised, documented, and described so that others — including your future self — can understand:

  • what was done and why;
  • how results were produced;
  • what assumptions and decisions shaped the work;
  • what could (and could not) be reused, and under what conditions.

Three foundations support this:

  • Documentation: READMEs, protocols, decision logs, and data dictionaries that capture context and rationale.
  • Organisation: consistent folder structures, naming conventions, and versioning that reduce ambiguity and error.
  • Metadata: structured descriptions that make outputs interpretable and discoverable (including for restricted-access data).

The next chapter introduces minimum expectations for describing outputs through the FAIR principles (see Chapter 2: FAIR basics).

AI and Open Science and Reproducibility

Automated and AI-assisted tools can improve efficiency and insight, but introduce new reproducibility challenges: opaque decision-making, stochastic outputs, undocumented prompts or parameters, and rapid model version changes. Open research practices remain essential to ensure transparency, accountability, and reuse in AI-assisted workflows.

What you will do in later modules

This course focuses on practical steps that reduce risk and increase continuity, including:

  • structuring data and materials for reuse (Chapter 2: FAIR basics);
  • organising projects so work remains legible and maintainable;
  • documenting decisions and workflows so results can be understood and defended;
  • improving code quality and reducing manual steps where appropriate;
  • preparing outputs for sharing, archiving, and attribution.

You are not expected to adopt everything at once. The course is designed to support incremental improvement.

Exercises (5–10 minutes)

  1. Choose one current or recent project you are involved in.
    Write down one thing that would make it hard for someone else to understand your results in six months’ time.
    Where would you record this? (e.g. project README, methods note, decision log)

  2. Identify one constraint (ethical, legal, contractual, or practical) that limits what you can share openly.
    Where is that constraint recorded now — and where should it be recorded? (e.g. DMP, ethics documentation, governance notes, project README)

If you are unsure what “good enough” looks like for these artefacts, see the relevant templates in Resources and Further Reading.

Key takeaways

  • Open research is now a baseline expectation, not an optional ideal.
  • Reproducibility problems usually arise from everyday workflow issues, not misconduct.
  • Proportionate openness reduces risk, inefficiency, and avoidable error.
  • You do not need fully open data to practise open research — but you do need clear documentation and defensible decisions.
  • Chapter 2: FAIR basics translates these ideas into concrete, data-level practice.
