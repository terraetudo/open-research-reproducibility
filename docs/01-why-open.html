Chapter 1 — Why Open Research and Reproducibility?

Chapter 1

Why Open Research and Reproducibility?

Open research is not about making everything public. It is about making research easier to understand, check, trust and reuse, within appropriate ethical, legal and practical boundaries.

What this chapter does

This chapter sets up the rest of the workshop. It explains why openness and reproducibility matter, what the key terms mean in practice, and why these issues are relevant even when working with sensitive or restricted-access data.

The aim is not to make every project perfectly open. The aim is to make everyday research workflows clearer, better documented, easier to check, and easier to hand over.

Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter, you should be able to:

  • explain why openness and reproducibility are now core expectations in research;
  • distinguish between open research, transparency and reproducibility;
  • recognise common sources of irreproducibility in everyday workflows;
  • understand how open practices can reduce risk, inefficiency and avoidable error;
  • identify how later chapters translate these principles into practical research habits.

This chapter is deliberately conceptual. Later chapters turn these ideas into practical steps: project organisation, data management planning, documentation, code quality, automation, publishing, persistent identifiers and reuse.

Why openness now?

Open research is no longer a niche movement or an optional add-on. Across research, concerns about irreproducible findings, undocumented analytical decisions and inaccessible outputs have led funders, journals and institutions to raise expectations around transparency and robustness1–3.

For most researchers, the issue is not ideology. It is practical risk.

Research projects often involve multiple datasets, changing teams, evolving analyses, sensitive information and informal decisions made under time pressure. Without clear organisation and documentation, it becomes difficult to explain what was done, why it was done and whether the same results could be produced again.

Open and reproducible practice helps reduce:

  • avoidable rework;
  • loss of project memory;
  • uncertainty about which file, script or output is current;
  • errors introduced through manual or undocumented steps;
  • barriers to collaboration, handover, publication and reuse.
A practical way to think about reproducibility

Could someone else understand what you did well enough to check it?

Could you understand it yourself if you came back to the project in six months?

If the answer is “probably not”, the workflow may need better organisation, clearer documentation or more explicit version control.

Reproducibility as a visible route

Reproducibility is sometimes framed narrowly as whether someone can re-run an analysis and get exactly the same result. That matters, but it is not the whole story.

A more useful starting point is whether someone with relevant expertise can follow the route taken through the research process: what was done, why it was done, what evidence was generated, what judgements were made, and how these led to the findings.

UKRN definition of reproducibility

The UK Reproducibility Network describes reproducibility as research being sufficiently transparent that someone with the relevant expertise can clearly follow:

  • how the research was done;
  • why it was done in that way;
  • the evidence that was established;
  • the reasoning and judgements that were used;
  • how these led justifiably to the research findings and conclusions.

Making the route visible

flowchart LR
    A[Research question] --> B[Data sources]
    B --> C[Data cleaning]
    C --> D[Analysis decisions]
    D --> E[Results]
    E --> F[Findings and conclusions]

    B -. document .-> G[Project record]
    C -. document .-> G
    D -. document .-> G
    E -. document .-> G

Without documentation, others may see only the final conclusion. Reproducible research makes the route to that conclusion visible.

Key terms used in this workshop

These terms overlap, but they are not identical.

Open research

Making research outputs, methods and decisions accessible to others, where this is lawful, ethical and appropriate.

Transparency

Clearly documenting what was done, why it was done and how decisions were made, including decisions that may not appear in the final paper.

Reproducibility

Enabling someone else — or your future self — to understand and, where possible, recreate the analytical steps and results using the same data and methods.

A project can be reproducible without being fully open. This is particularly important for research using sensitive, confidential or linked administrative data. Conversely, sharing outputs is not enough for reproducibility if key steps, assumptions, software, metadata or decisions are missing.

Reproducible is not the only “-ible”

The Turing Way distinguishes between several related concepts4.

Different ways research can be checked or extended

Same analysis Different analysis
Same data Reproducible Robust
Different data Replicable Generalisable

These distinctions help clarify what kind of checking is possible. Not every project can support every form of reuse, especially when data are sensitive, access-controlled or context-specific.

Reproducible

Same data, same analysis.

Replicable

Different data, same analysis.

Robust

Same data, different analysis.

Generalisable

Different data, different analysis.

The important point is to be clear about what kind of checking, reuse or scrutiny is realistic.

Principles, practices and tools

This workshop separates principles, practices and tools.

Principles

The underlying aims, such as transparency, accountability, reuse, quality and trust.

Practices

The behaviours that implement those aims, such as documentation, version control, naming conventions and decision logs.

Tools

The software, repositories and platforms that support the practices, such as RStudio, Quarto, Git, GitHub, OSF or institutional repositories.

Tools change quickly. Good practices travel across projects, disciplines and institutions. This is why the workshop focuses on habits and workflows, not only on any single platform.

Minimum expectations in practice

Open research is increasingly treated as a default position, applied across the research lifecycle and implemented proportionately within ethical, legal and contractual constraints.

In most UCL and UK-funded research contexts, you should assume that the following expectations may apply somewhere in your project:

  • plan data management proportionately, and document decisions;
  • make outputs findable and preservable, including data, code, protocols, materials or metadata where appropriate;
  • enable open access to publications through institutional and funder-compliant routes;
  • record constraints explicitly, including sensitive data, governance restrictions, intellectual property or contractual limits;
  • document how restrictions affect sharing, access, reuse and retention.

The key point is proportionality. Open research does not mean treating all projects in the same way. It means making defensible decisions and recording them clearly.

Open does not always mean public

For sensitive data, good open research may mean clear metadata, documented access routes, transparent governance, synthetic examples, preserved code and well-described restrictions.

Where irreproducibility comes from

Irreproducibility is usually not caused by misconduct. More often, it comes from ordinary workflow problems.

Common examples include:

  • manual data cleaning steps that are not recorded;
  • unclear file names, folder structures or version histories;
  • results generated interactively without a saved script;
  • missing information about software versions, packages or dependencies;
  • undocumented exclusions, recoding decisions or sensitivity analyses;
  • project knowledge held by one person and lost when they move on.

These problems often become visible late: during submission, peer review, audit, collaboration or handover. By then, they are harder and more expensive to fix.

Where reproducibility can break down

flowchart LR
    A[Generate research question] --> B[Design study]
    B --> C[Collect or access data]
    C --> D[Clean and analyse data]
    D --> E[Interpret results]
    E --> F[Publish and reuse]

    B -. bias or weak design .-> X[Risk to reproducibility]
    C -. missing provenance .-> X
    D -. undocumented cleaning or p-hacking .-> X
    E -. selective interpretation .-> X
    F -. inaccessible materials .-> X

Some risks are methodological, such as poor design or low statistical power. Others are workflow risks, such as missing documentation, unclear data provenance or inaccessible materials2.

Common risk point

If your workflow depends on remembering what you did, rather than recording it somewhere stable, it is fragile.

Barriers are real

Working reproducibly takes time, skills and support. Barriers can include lack of incentives, fear of being held to higher standards than others, limited recognition in promotion processes, complex data governance, large computational infrastructure, and uncertainty about what can be shared.

This workshop will not fix all of those structural issues. It focuses on practical improvements that are within the control of individual researchers and teams, while recognising that institutions, funders, journals and senior leaders also have responsibilities.

Quick discussion

Which barrier is most relevant to your own work at the moment?

Is it mainly a personal workflow issue, a team issue, an institutional issue, or a data governance issue?

Why this helps you too

Open and reproducible practice is often presented as something researchers do for other people: reviewers, funders, journals, collaborators or future users of the work. That is true, but it is also useful for you.

Avoid disaster

Clear records make it less likely that an analysis becomes impossible to explain, repeat or defend later.

Write papers more easily

Methods, exclusions, decisions and outputs are easier to report when they have been recorded during the project.

Support review and revision

Reviewers, supervisors and collaborators can assess the work more efficiently when the workflow is transparent.

Enable continuity

A well-documented project is easier to hand over when someone moves role, changes institution or returns after a break.

Build reputation

Reliable, well-organised work increases trust in the research and in the team producing it.

Open research in day-to-day work

Open research is not only about making outputs publicly available at the end of a project. Across the research lifecycle, work should be organised, documented and described so that others — including your future self — can understand:

  • what was done;
  • why it was done;
  • how results were produced;
  • what assumptions and decisions shaped the work;
  • what could and could not be reused;
  • what restrictions apply.

Three foundations support this.

Documentation

READMEs, protocols, decision logs, metadata, data dictionaries and codebooks that capture context and rationale.

Organisation

Consistent folder structures, file names and versioning that reduce ambiguity and error.

Description

Clear metadata and access information so that outputs are interpretable, discoverable and reusable where appropriate.

These foundations are particularly important for collaborative, longitudinal or sensitive-data projects, where people may join or leave the team and not all materials can be made publicly available.

What can be made open?

Open research is not a single action. Different parts of the research process can be made more visible, reusable or accessible.

Publications

Open access articles, reports, preprints or plain-language summaries.

Data and metadata

Open data where possible; metadata, documentation and access conditions where data are restricted.

Code and software

Scripts, notebooks, packages, workflows and dependency information.

Methods and materials

Protocols, analysis plans, survey instruments, SOPs and decision logs.

Review and interpretation

Transparent peer review, reporting checklists, sensitivity analyses and clear statements of limitations.

AI and reproducibility

Automated and AI-assisted tools can support research workflows, for example by helping with coding, summarising, drafting, checking or exploring ideas. They can also introduce reproducibility challenges.

These include:

  • outputs that vary across prompts, sessions or model versions;
  • unclear provenance of generated text, code or summaries;
  • hidden assumptions in suggested analyses;
  • difficulty reconstructing how an output was produced;
  • risk of over-reliance on plausible but incorrect outputs.

The same reproducibility principles still apply: document important decisions, keep records of prompts or generated code where relevant, check outputs critically, and make clear where AI-assisted tools have materially shaped the work.

Activity: the six-month test

Individual reflection or small-group discussion

Choose one current or recent project.

Write down one thing that would make it difficult for someone else to understand your results in six months’ time.

Then ask:

  • Where is this information recorded now?
  • Would someone else know where to find it?
  • Would your future self understand it?
  • What small change would reduce the risk?

Activity: constraints are part of the workflow

Sensitive or restricted data

Identify one constraint that limits what you can share openly. This might be ethical, legal, contractual, governance-related or practical.

Then ask:

  • Where is that constraint recorded?
  • Does it explain what can be shared, what cannot be shared and why?
  • Does it explain how someone could request access, if access is possible?
  • Is the restriction visible to future project members?

What comes next?

Later chapters translate these ideas into practical steps:

  • describing outputs using FAIR principles (Chapter 2: FAIR basics);
  • organising projects so work remains legible and maintainable;
  • documenting decisions and workflows so results can be understood and defended;
  • improving code quality and reducing manual steps where appropriate;
  • preparing outputs for sharing, archiving, attribution and reuse.

You are not expected to change everything at once. The workshop is designed to support incremental improvement: making each project a little easier to understand, check and build on.

Key takeaways

  • Open research is now a baseline expectation, not an optional ideal.
  • Reproducibility problems usually arise from everyday workflow issues, not misconduct.
  • Sensitive or restricted data can still be handled in transparent and reproducible ways.
  • Good documentation reduces risk, improves continuity and supports reuse.
  • The aim is not perfection, but proportionate and defensible practice.
